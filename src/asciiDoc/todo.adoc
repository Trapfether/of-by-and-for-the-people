= Constitutional Draft TODO
Andrew Trefethen
:toc: left
:toclevels: 3

== Purpose
This document tracks major constitutional concepts and operating-system
details that should be considered for future drafting. It is based on a
comparison between the current proposal and the Constitution of the
United States.

The checklist is not a command to copy the existing Constitution. Some
items may be rejected, replaced, or intentionally redesigned. The goal
is to make sure each area is consciously addressed rather than missed by
accident.

== Checklist

=== Congressional Structure

* [ ] Define House membership, terms, districts, and apportionment.
* [ ] Define rules for House vacancies.
* [ ] Define Senate terms, classes, qualifications, and vacancies.
* [ ] Define quorum rules for each chamber.
* [ ] Define how each chamber chooses officers.
* [ ] Define internal discipline and expulsion procedures.
* [ ] Define compensation rules for members of Congress.
* [ ] Decide whether legislative debate receives immunity from ordinary
legal retaliation.
+
The current draft creates Congress, the House, and the Senate, but it
does not yet fully define how those bodies are populated, how long
members serve, how vacancies are handled, or how each chamber conducts
basic internal business.

=== Enumerated Legislative Powers

* [ ] Borrowing money and managing public debt.
* [ ] Regulating interstate commerce.
* [ ] Regulating foreign commerce.
* [ ] Naturalization and immigration.
* [ ] Bankruptcy.
* [ ] Currency, coinage, and counterfeiting.
* [ ] Weights and measures.
* [ ] Postal systems.
* [ ] Patents, copyrights, and other limited intellectual-property
systems.
* [ ] Creation of federal courts below the Supreme Court.
* [ ] Piracy and offenses against international law.
* [ ] Declaration of war.
* [ ] Raising and supporting military forces.
* [ ] Militia, reserve, or national guard rules.
* [ ] Governing federal property, territories, and the capital district.
* [ ] General implementation authority similar to a "necessary and
proper" clause.
+
A constitution should make clear which powers are granted to the
legislature. Without an enumerated-powers article, later courts and
officials may have too little guidance about what Congress can and
cannot do.

=== Executive Structure and Powers

* [ ] Define how the President is elected.
* [ ] Define presidential term length.
* [ ] Decide whether presidential term limits exist.
* [ ] Define qualifications for President and Vice President.
* [ ] Define succession and incapacity procedures.
* [ ] Define commander-in-chief authority.
* [ ] Define appointment power.
* [ ] Define treaty power.
* [ ] Define ambassador and foreign-relations authority.
* [ ] Define periodic reporting duties, such as a State of the Union or
similar public report.
* [ ] Decide whether a pardon power exists and, if so, its limits.
* [ ] Define the duty to faithfully execute the laws.
* [ ] Define impeachment or removal procedure separate from Revocation
Votes, if desired.
+
The current draft names a President and Vice President, but many
ordinary executive powers and limits remain undefined. These details
matter because executive ambiguity tends to be exploited during crises.

=== Judicial Structure

* [ ] Define judicial appointment and confirmation.
* [ ] Define judicial tenure or term limits.
* [ ] Define judicial compensation protections.
* [ ] Define Supreme Court size or the process for setting its size.
* [ ] Define federal court jurisdiction.
* [ ] Distinguish original jurisdiction from appellate jurisdiction.
* [ ] Define the right to trial by jury.
* [ ] Define treason and its evidentiary standard.
+
The proposal has judicial review, but the judiciary also needs an
institutional design: who becomes a judge, how long judges serve, what
cases federal courts may hear, and what trial protections apply.

=== Individual Rights

* [ ] Religion, including free exercise and establishment limits.
* [ ] Freedom of the press.
* [ ] Assembly.
* [ ] Petitioning the government.
* [ ] Arms.
* [ ] Quartering of soldiers.
* [ ] Search and seizure.
* [ ] Warrants and probable cause.
* [ ] Due process.
* [ ] Takings of property and compensation.
* [ ] Self-incrimination.
* [ ] Double jeopardy.
* [ ] Right to counsel.
* [ ] Speedy and public trial.
* [ ] Confrontation of witnesses.
* [ ] Compulsory process for obtaining witnesses.
* [ ] Civil jury trials.
* [ ] Excessive bail.
* [ ] Excessive fines.
* [ ] Cruel and unusual punishment beyond the death penalty ban.
* [ ] Unenumerated rights.
* [ ] Reservation of powers to the people or to states.
+
The draft strongly protects political speech and bans capital
punishment, but many ordinary criminal-procedure, civil-procedure, and
personal-liberty protections still need explicit treatment.

=== Equal Citizenship and Civil Rights

* [ ] Define citizenship by birth.
* [ ] Define naturalized citizenship.
* [ ] Equal protection.
* [ ] Due process against state governments.
* [ ] Voting-rights protections by race.
* [ ] Voting-rights protections by sex.
* [ ] Voting-rights protections by age, where applicable.
* [ ] Voting-rights protections against poll taxes or wealth barriers.
* [ ] Voting-rights protections against prior servitude or similar
status-based exclusions.
* [ ] Broader anti-discrimination guarantees, if desired.
+
The current draft discusses citizens and voting eligibility, but does
not yet fully define who is a citizen or which equality rules bind
federal, state, and local government.

=== Federalism

* [ ] Supremacy of the Constitution and valid federal law.
* [ ] Full faith and credit between states.
* [ ] Privileges and immunities between states.
* [ ] Extradition between states.
* [ ] Admission of new states.
* [ ] Changes to state boundaries.
* [ ] Guarantee of republican government or another minimum state
government standard.
* [ ] Federal protection against invasion.
* [ ] Limits on state treaties.
* [ ] Limits on state currency.
* [ ] Limits on state tariffs.
* [ ] Limits on state war powers.
* [ ] Limits on interstate compacts.
+
The current draft references federal, state, and local government, but
the relationship among them still needs rules. Federalism rules prevent
confusion about which government controls when legal systems overlap.

=== Fiscal Rules

* [ ] Appropriations requirement before public money is spent.
* [ ] Public accounting of revenue and expenditures.
* [ ] Debt authority and debt limits, if any.
* [ ] Uniformity rules for federal taxes.
* [ ] Export tax limits, if desired.
* [ ] Limits on drawing money from the treasury.
* [ ] Budget process.
* [ ] Emergency spending rules.
+
The tax and benefits article sets important principles for continuity
and incentives, but the constitution also needs mechanical rules for how
money is authorized, spent, audited, borrowed, and reported.

=== Foreign Affairs and War

* [ ] Treaty process.
* [ ] Ambassador appointments and duties.
* [ ] Recognition of foreign governments.
* [ ] Declaration of war.
* [ ] Military funding limits.
* [ ] Civilian control of the military outside martial law.
* [ ] Division of war powers between Congress and the President.
+
Emergency powers and martial law are addressed, but ordinary foreign
affairs and military authority still need a peacetime structure.

=== Constitutional Amendment and Ratification

* [ ] Who may propose amendments.
* [ ] Vote thresholds for proposing amendments.
* [ ] Ratification method.
* [ ] Public vote requirements, if any.
* [ ] State approval requirements, if any.
* [ ] Constitutional convention process.
* [ ] Time limits for ratification.
* [ ] List of unamendable clauses.
+
Some articles already say they cannot be amended, but the draft needs a
general amendment process for everything else.

=== Public Office Rules

* [ ] Oath of office.
* [ ] Incompatibility of offices.
* [ ] Emoluments, gifts, and foreign payments.
* [ ] Bribery limits.
* [ ] Titles of nobility.
* [ ] Residency requirements.
* [ ] Age requirements.
* [ ] Citizenship requirements.
* [ ] Financial disclosure.
* [ ] Conflict-of-interest rules.
+
Public-office rules help define who may hold power and what outside
interests are incompatible with public duty.

=== Transition and Continuity

* [ ] Effective date.
* [ ] Continuity of existing laws.
* [ ] Continuity of courts.
* [ ] Continuity of agencies.
* [ ] Treatment of existing public debts.
* [ ] Treatment of current officeholders.
* [ ] First elections under the new constitution.
* [ ] First appointments under the new constitution.
+
If this proposal replaces an existing constitutional system, it needs a
transition article. Without one, even a well-designed system can become
uncertain at the moment it takes effect.
